DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS

  • Psoriasis
  • Tinea Corporis
  • Allergic Contact Dermatitis
  • Irritant Contact Dermatitis
  • Mycosis Fungoides
  • Immunodeficiency
  • Scabies

CAUSES

  • Respiratory allergy: house dust mites, pollen and animal epithelia
  • Food allergy: more frequent in infants and children; cow milk, eggs, fish, soy or peanuts
  • Microbial agents: Staphylococcus aureus
  • Dry skin: long bathing, cold dry climate, insufficient use of emollients
  • Itching and subsequent scratching
  • Sweating: impermeable clothing, hot work places, stress
  • Chemical/physical irritants: smoking, clothes
  • Psychological stress

SYMPTOMS

  • increased susceptibility to allergic rhinitis and asthma
  • Lichenification: excessive itching which leads to thick, leathery patches of skin
  • Excoriation: repeated picking at one's own skin
  • Crusting
  • typically symmetric, chronic relapsing course
  • involving mainly the face, neck and the flexural surface of the elbow and knee

PREVENTIVE TREATMENT

  • Moisturisers to stop the skin from becoming dry
  • Try to reduce scratching whenever possible
  • Keep nails short and clean to minimize damage to the skin from unintentional scratching
  • Avoid triggers that can cause eczema flare-upsg

WHEN TO SEE A DOCTOR

  • When a bacterial infection occurs:
    • fluid oozing from the skin
    • a yellow crust on the surface
    • small yellowish-white spots in the eczema
    • swollen and sore
    • high temperature (fever)
  • It is possible for the eczema to be infected with the herpes simplex virus, which can develop into eczema herpeticum
    • areas of painful eczema that quickly get worse
    • fluid-filled blisters that break open and leave small, shallow open sores
    • high temperature (fever)
